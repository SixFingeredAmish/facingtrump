Seattle, WA 9 – 10 November 2016

Facing Trump

On November 9th, Americans woke up to a looming Trump presidency, many were scared, angry, and confused. Some felt like strangers in their own country, stunned at why many found Trump so appealing. Here, Seattleites talk about how they feel and what the election means to them.

11:26 am ∙ 9 November

People will just listen to the soundbites and they won’t look into the details. So Trump goes and tells the coal miners in West Virginia that he’s going to bring their jobs back. It ain’t happening.

Patrick, age ∙ Willows, CA

11:58 am ∙ 9 November

I have no idea what the next four years are going to be like. You know what they say, ‘hope is the last one to go.’

I just hope he surprises me. I really hope he surprises me.

Julio, age ∙ Portugal

But when that happens it’s an opportunity to talk about it with them and it’s good. Kids know it and feel that it’s a heavy topic and they want to understand. 1:19 pm ∙ 9 November

We’d like to think that the Klan and all that is behind us but Trump just really came out and energized that. With all he’s said and the demographics, you’d think Hillary would have won handily.

I want to go work in law enforcement but with someone like Donald Trump and with the influence he has, I’d be scared of doing racial profiling. That’s going to completely change any progress that I want to be a part of.

3:11 pm ∙ 9 November

Adam, 26 ∙ Mill Valley, CA

3:19 pm ∙ 9 November

I think Trump makes it unsafe for many people to go about conducting their daily lives. I’m a queer woman and I’m afraid. I broke down crying last night when the race was called and my roommate came over and hugged me and said, ‘I won’t let them take you’ which I feel is not a sentence that should have to be said in this century and this country.

I think we’ve got a very long and journey ahead of us that is going to cause unprecedented levels of fear and hatred that are going to continue to rise. I want to say that I have a lot of hope for the future but I have a lot of fear for the future as well.

Zoe, 20 ∙ Seattle, WA

3:33 pm ∙ 9 November

I came to America to study financial mathematics. If you want to work in a bank and be an analyst there’s nowhere else to study right now. After Brexit, London is longer be the economic center in Europe. And I don’t know what will happen to America and to my chances of working here after I graduate. Maybe I’ll have to go to Canada for grad school.

Tom, age ∙ China

8:10 pm ∙ 9 November

In spite of it all, the younger generation all voted very blue throughout the nation so that means it’s very much like Brexit where the older generation wanted to divorce themselves from the rest of Europe and the young people wanted to stay with Europe. So unlike Brexit, you can’t reverse that but here in four years they can change that.

People will speak up their mind a lot more instead of being somewhat reserved and thinking that they can’t talk. I think they will definitely talk a lot more about what their rights are and how things should be done. It’s going to get people out of their insecurities and stand by equality, that’s the positive thing.
